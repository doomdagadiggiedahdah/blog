Quick Thoughts on ZK Proof Foundations

A friendly exploration of zero-knowledge proof basics through dialogue

Intro

I like holding hands. This is a hand-holding post on the basic ideas of what makes zero-knowledge proofs work.

Dialogue

Question: So like, what is it?

Answer: Zero knowledge proof, like a proof but without needing to reveal that baseline info.

Question: What?

Answer: Yeah I know.

Question: But how?

Answer: Well, I think saying that "I have proof this is true" is actually a worse way to say this. Instead, it's better to say "it's probably true".

Question: .......

Answer: Quick background; there's two people in this equation, a prover (the one saying "believe me!") and the verifier (the one saying, "but how do I actually know?"). The prover wants the verifier to believe them, but doesn't want to give out some type of information. (let's just say the prover doesn't want to share their birthdate, but wants to go a club; they need to prove that they're over 21.)

Question: Ok, prover needs to show they're over 21, but doesn't want to show their birthday.

Answer: Yup. Think, "proof = true or false", keep hidden info

Question: Great

Answer: Great

Question: But wait, proof = true or false, but you said it's better to say "probably true"?

Answer: Ya, so what happens with some fancy computer magic is the verifier (the one saying "can I actually believe the prover?") will give a bunch of True/False tests to the prover. These tests basically say (in computer terms) "ok, if you actually were over 21, then you should pass this test!" The prover can now 1. either guess correctly each time (which sounds hard to do maybe), or they 2. actually are over 21 and you can pass the test.

Question: But wouldn't the answer just be "yes" each time? That seems pretty easy to hack

Answer: Yeah that would, so they change it up a bit. basically though, if you have the ground truth, if you are over 21, it's easy to answer correctly. If you don't, you just have to guess. You have to guess right each time they ask you a question

Question: Each time?

Answer: Ya, imagine having to guess correctly a whole bunch of times, and if you mess up once, you're done kiddo

Question: Oh my

Answer: And this is also where "probably true" comes in. If someone guessed it right once, that's only 50% chance (true/false). But what if they had to get it right twice? That's 25% (50% x 50%). Three times? 12.5%.

Question: And it just keeps going down?

Answer: Yup, put into your calculator (1/2)^{the number of tests you'd give}. (first try the above numbers, noting that you have to move the decimal two places to the right to get percentages like above. (1/2)^1 = .5 , but move it two and you've got 50%)

Intermission

You should actually plug some numbers into the calculator to see how quickly the number goes down. Ask yourself this, if you were the verifier, what would your acceptable percentage be? How small of a chance would you accept of some rando correctly guessing each of your tests? How small does it have to be to accept that guessing correctly probably didn't happen?

Back to the show

Question: Wow, ten tests gives a 0.0976562% of someone guessing correctly, or 1/1024 chance of getting it right.

Answer: Crazy right! And now again imagine yourself as the verifier. At what percentage do you say that you're convinced? The actual percentage is something fun to think about, but the more important thing is to realize that the verifier actually never sees the evidence of whether or not someone is lying, but instead the verifier sees a low enough probability that they're not lying.

Question: Ohhhhh

Answer: Yeahhhhhhh. And just to wrap it up: the verifier never actually knows that the prover is telling the truth (this is a zero knowledge proof). The verifier is just convinced at some point that it is.
